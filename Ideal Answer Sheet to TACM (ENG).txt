Puzzle 2: The Weighted Paradox
Answer: It is logically impossible.


Reasoning:
1.  Information Theory: I approached this as a constraints problem. You have a balance scale with three possible outcomes per weighing: Left Heavy, Right Heavy, or Balanced.
2.  State Space: There are 3 coins, and any one of them could be Heavy or Light. This creates 3 × 2 = 6 possible states (Coin A is Heavy, Coin A is Light, Coin B is Heavy, etc.).
3.  **The Constraint:** A single weighing provides $\log_2(3)$ bits of information (one of three outcomes). You need to distinguish between 6 distinct possibilities. Mathematically, 3 < 6. You cannot map 6 distinct inputs to 3 distinct outputs without ambiguity.
4.  Scenario Check:
    *   If you weigh A vs. B and they balance, you know C is the odd one. However, you have used your one weighing. You have no way to determine if C is heavier or lighter than the others without a reference weighing.
    *   Therefore, the "Paradox" is that the puzzle assumes a solution exists, but the elegant solution is the proof that it does not (under standard physical laws).

Puzzle 3: Semantic Prime
Answer: TWELVE


Reasoning:
1.  Analyzing the Sequence: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12. These are sequential even numbers.
2.  The Trap: Mathematically it suggests the next number is 14. Pure pattern matching suggests 24 (doubling 12). The prompt explicitly forbids 24.
3.  Linguistic Method:I looked for a pattern where the sequence is finite or cyclical. The most common "semantic" system that counts by evens and ends at 12/24 is a Clock.
4.  The Logic: On a clock face, the numbers progress 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12. The logical "Twelfth" element in a continuous sequence of these strikes would be the 12th position.
    *   Pass 1: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12 (6 elements).
    *   Pass 2: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12 (6 elements).
    *   The 12th element is always 12 (Twelve).
5.  Why this fits: It satisfies the "Linguistic" (semantic meaning of time), "Self-referential" (clocks refer to their own cycle), and strictly explains why the sequence doesn't proceed to 14 or 24 linearly.

Puzzle 4: The Code
Answer: 66


Reasoning:
1.  Vertical Analysis:
    *   Column A: 1, 2, 3, 4 (Linear increase).
    *   Column B: 2, 5, 10... Formula seems to be $A^2 + 1$.
        *   $1^2+1 = 2$
        *   $2^2+1 = 5$
        *   $3^2+1 = 10$
        *   (Row 3 check: $4^2+1 = 17$. Matches).
2.  Finding Column C:
    *   Row 1: $A=2, C=10$. ($2^3 + 2 = 10$).
    *   Row 2: $A=3, C=29$. ($3^3 + 2 = 29$).
3.  Testing Consistency: The pattern must hold for Row 3.
    *   $A = 4$.
    *   Formula: $A^3 + 2$.
    *   $4^3 (64) + 2 = 66$.
4.  Verification: also checked for a Row-based relationship.
    *   Relationship: $(A \times B) - (Row number - 1) = C$.
    *   Row 1: $(2 \times 5) - 0 = 10$.
    *   Row 2: $(3 \times 10) - 1 = 29$.
    *   Row 3: $(4 \times 17) - 2 = 66$.
    *   Both methods yield 66.
